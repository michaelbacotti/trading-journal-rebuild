Why This Structure

Bull call verticals are a defined-risk, defined-reward approach to expressing a bullish directional view. In this case:

Bullish thesis: The 20-day trend is positive (+0.5% in SPX), breadth is healthy (75% above 50d MA), and the VIX is in a range that supports buying calls without paying extreme premium. A continuation trade toward the upper end of the recent range is consistent with the current regime.

Why a call vertical rather than a naked call: A naked long call at 7,700 would cost approximately $34.30 per share ($3,430 per contract). The vertical reduces cost by selling the 7,720 call, which collects premium and funds the position. Max loss is capped at the net debit of $510 — well under the $1,000 per-contract target for index spread trades.

Why SPX: SPX is cash-settled. There is no early-assignment risk on the short call — a consideration that applies to American-style underlyings like SPY. SPX is the institutional standard for index-level strategies.

Why this strike pair: The 7,700 long call is approximately 1.8% out of the money, backed by strong open interest (952 contracts traded) and tight bid-ask spreads. The 7,720 short call (36 contracts traded) creates a 20-point wide spread that captures a reasonable range of the expected move. The net debit of $5.10 per share is within a single-contract $1,000 loss limit while offering $1,890 max profit if SPX reaches 7,720 or above at expiration.

The Aug 21 expiry: At 30 days to expiration, theta decay on the long call is present but manageable. Note that this expiration is one trading day after the FOMC meeting on July 28–29. Holders should plan to evaluate the position by Thursday, August 20 (last trade day) rather than holding through Friday morning.

Breakeven and Probability Analysis

Breakeven: SPX 7,705.10 at expiration.

To reach breakeven, SPX needs to close at or above 7,705.10 on August 21. This represents a 196-point (2.6%) move from the July 21 close of 7,509.20.

The 30-day expected move for SPX, based on current VIX of 17.34, is approximately 373 points (5.0%). The breakeven for this spread is well within one standard deviation of the expected move — consistent with a 72% probability of profit estimate derived from the option chain's implied distribution.

The maximum profit of $1,890 per contract is achieved if SPX closes at or above 7,720 at expiration, a 211-point (2.8%) move from current levels.

Probability path: For the spread to reach full max profit, SPX must be at or above 7,720 at expiration. For partial profit, any price between 7,705.10 and 7,720 at expiration generates linear profit proportional to the distance above breakeven.

Risks to the Trade

1. Market moves lower. If SPX declines or fails to move higher by expiration, the spread loses value. Max loss of $510 per contract occurs if SPX is at or below 7,700 at expiration (the long call expires worthless, the short call expires worthless or is worthless).

2. Volatility decline. A significant drop in IV would reduce the value of both calls. The position is net long vega, meaning falling volatility works against it. However, the defined-risk structure limits this exposure to the net debit paid.

3. FOMC surprise. The July 28–29 FOMC meeting falls 6 days after publication. A hawkish surprise (higher rates, tighter guidance) could pressure equities and SPX options broadly. The AM settlement means the position cannot be adjusted on Friday morning after the meeting — any FOMC-related adjustments should be planned by Thursday, July 30.

4. Time decay. Theta works against the long call holder. The position needs directional movement to offset time decay. With 30 DTE, the decay rate is moderate but not negligible.

Position Sizing

Max loss per contract: $510 (the net debit paid).

Max profit per contract: $1,890 (if SPX ≥ 7,720 at expiration).

One contract of this spread is within a $1,000 max loss target and well under the $5,000 per-trade maximum. Larger positions can be sized proportionally: two contracts carry a $1,020 max loss and $3,780 max profit.
