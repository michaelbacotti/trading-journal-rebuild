What the market did Friday

SPY closed at $743.29 on Friday 2026-07-17, down 0.99% on the day. The YTD return sits at +9.00%, the trailing twelve-month at +18.44%. Volatility is contained — the VIX is in the mid-teens and SPY's option chain is pricing roughly 1.0–1.4% one-day expected moves at the front of the curve. This is a low-IV regime. The Playbook's Section 3 says it plainly: when IV rank is in the 0–20 band, the favored structure is debit spreads and single-leg longs, not premium-selling structures. Selling premium here would be wrong on the regime alone — the edge isn't there.

The thesis

The S&P 500 has spent the last three weeks inside an 18-point range (roughly $730–$748). Breadth is constructive, mega-cap tech has held, and the macro tape is a slow grind higher rather than a melt-up. None of that argues for a directional breakout to the upside with conviction. What it argues for is a defined-risk bullish expression that benefits if the range resolves up, doesn't bleed badly if it chops sideways, and has a clear exit if it breaks down — all while paying for itself with theta rather than fighting it.

That's a vertical debit spread, sized small, with a short strike inside the range and a long strike far enough out to keep the debit manageable.

The structure

SPY 14–21 DTE bull call spread:

Leg Action Strike Expiry Estimated Cost
Long Buy 1 $730 21 DTE (~Aug 8, 2026) ~$15.40
Short Sell 1 $745 21 DTE (~Aug 8, 2026) ~$3.90
Net debit ~$11.50

That's roughly $1,150 per contract of defined risk. Max profit is hit at expiration with SPY at or above $745 — the spread is worth $15.00 wide at that point, so max gain is $3.50 ($350/contract). Max loss is the full $1,150 debit if SPY closes below $730 at expiration. Reward-to-risk is roughly 1 : 3.3 — that's intentional at low IV, where the probability of the structure paying out is the premium you're buying with the debit.

Why this structure over the alternatives

Why not a short put vertical or iron condor? At IV rank 18–24, premium-selling structures on SPY are mathematically working against you. The IV-risk premium (the gap between implied and realized vol) is the engine that makes credit spreads profitable, and it's compressed in this regime. Selling premium here means taking a defined-risk short-vol position into an environment that doesn't reward it — the win rate is in the 50s, but the tail payoffs are punishing when they come.

Why not a same-week 7-DTE vertical? 7 DTE increases gamma risk and reduces the time available for the thesis to play out. The Playbook's sigma-distance table for 14-DTE vertical debit spreads calls for the short strike at 0.3–0.5σ — at SPY $743 with 14% IV over 14 days, that puts the short strike in the $747–$752 zone. Going shorter-dated forces a wider spread to keep the same sigma anchor, which inflates the debit without improving the probability enough to compensate.

Why not a calendar or diagonal? Both work at low IV, but they introduce a second expiry to manage and a skew dimension that the Playbook's Section 5 treats carefully. For a "Monday morning, size it once, manage it once" position, a single-expiry vertical is the cleanest expression. Calendars and diagonals come on Thursdays in the structure-deep-dive slot.

What time decay is doing for you (and against you)

The long $730C carries roughly –$15 to –$22/day of theta at 21 DTE, depending on the IV skew. The short $745C is roughly –$3 to –$5/day. Net theta on the spread at entry is about –$18 to –$27 per day, or roughly –$0.20 per contract per day on the $1,150 risk. That's the cost of carry. Over a 21-day hold the structure bleeds about $4–$6 per contract in pure time decay — meaning SPY only needs to drift up about 0.3–0.4% by expiration to recover the bleed and lock a small profit.

This is theta-positive on the short leg, theta-negative on the long leg, net modestly negative — which is exactly what a debit spread should be. The Playbook's sigma-distance framing is what makes this trade viable: at 0.3–0.5σ short strike, the daily bleed is dominated by the directional move, not by time passing.
