Why SPX, Not SPY or Sector ETFs

SPX is cash-settled European-style. There is no scenario where the short put is assigned — settlement is in cash at expiry based on the index level.

SPY is American-style. Short puts on SPY carry early-assignment risk on ex-dividend dates and in fast-market scenarios. Credit on SPY is similar to SPX, but the operational risk is higher.

XLK and other equity-sector options are American-style and trade with wider bid-ask spreads. For a $190-credit position, the spread differential materially changes the fill.

For a defined-risk index-level structure, SPX is the default.

Why This Structure

  • Theta-positive. Time decay works in favor of the position as long as SPX stays above the short strike.
  • Defined risk. Maximum loss is the width of the spread minus the credit. There is no scenario where the loss exceeds the spread width.
  • Sized small. One contract = $810 max loss, under the $1,000 default position-size cap.
  • IV-friendly. Near-term implied volatility (VIX 18.77) is above the mid-June range. Selling into elevated IV is more attractive than selling into a vol crush.
  • FOMC-comfortable. The structure has a 322-point buffer to the short strike, well beyond the 1σ 30-day expected move of ±278 points. A 9-day window to the FOMC decision is the right timeframe to be short premium into a known catalyst — long enough to collect meaningful theta, short enough that the IV term structure still prices the event.

Why Not the Alternatives

SPY bull put spread at the same delta. Same structure, same delta, similar credit. The only difference is early-assignment risk on the short put. The credit differential rarely justifies the operational risk on a small-size position. SPX is the default for index-level plays.

XLK bull call vertical. XLK was the worst-performing sector last week (−5.5% over 5 sessions). The bull-call vertical expresses a directional buy-the-dip view on Tech. The catalyst that drove XLK lower (rate-sensitivity into FOMC, geopolitical risk-off) is still present. Buying Tech calls into FOMC is fighting the catalyst.

Iron condor on SPX. Iron condors are appropriate in range-bound regimes with elevated implied volatility. The current regime is BULL with a defensive rotation, not range-bound. Iron condors cap both sides of the move; the bull put spread keeps the upside open.

Long SPX put or put spread. A standalone bearish structure. The breadth data (75% above 50d MA) and 20-day positive drift (+0.57%) do not support a directional decline thesis here.

Long volatility (straddle or strangle). Long-vol structures benefit from a vol spike. The term structure is in mild contango (0.91), meaning the market is not positioned for a sustained vol spike. Long vol here is fighting the term-structure signal.

Trade Management

Following the standard SOP from the Playbook:

  • Profit-take: Close at 50% of max profit (~$95 credit remaining, or $95 per contract).
  • Stop-loss: Close at 2× the credit received in loss (~$380 debit, or $380 per contract).
  • Time stop: Close at 7 DTE if neither profit-take nor stop-loss has triggered.
  • Adjustment: If SPX drops within 1% of the short strike ($7,135 × 0.99 = $7,064) with 14+ DTE remaining, roll the short strike down 5 points and out 7 days for a credit. Do not roll down without collecting a credit.

Risks to the Trade

  • FOMC surprise. A 50 bps cut would invert the term structure. The position can still work as long as SPX stays above the short strike, but plan to manage through the event rather than hold to expiry.
  • Iran escalation. A shipping disruption in the Strait of Hormuz could push Brent above $100 and trigger a broader risk-off move. Position sizing already accounts for tail risk at $810 max loss per contract.
  • Tech earnings. Several megacap technology companies report this week. An earnings miss from a large-cap name could drag XLK and SPX together. The short strike is 322 points below spot, which absorbs a single-name miss but not a sector-wide repricing.
