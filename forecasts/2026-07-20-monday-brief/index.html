Why SPX, and Not SPY or XLK

This is the most important choice in the article, and it is one that many retail traders get wrong.

SPX is cash-settled. When the option expires, settlement is in cash based on the index level. There is no scenario where you are assigned shares of the underlying index. This eliminates early-assignment risk entirely.

SPY is American-style. If you are short a SPY put and SPY gaps down hard into an event (FOMC, earnings, geopolitical shock), there is a small but real risk of early assignment on the ex-dividend date or in a fast-market scenario. The credit on SPY is similar to SPX, but the operational risk is meaningfully higher.

XLK and other equity-sector options carry the same American-style assignment risk as SPY, plus they have wider bid-ask spreads and lower option volume than the index products. For institutional option strategies, the choice is straightforward: SPX or XSP for index-level plays.

Why This Structure Works Here

The bull put spread is the right expression for a BULL regime with elevated near-term implied volatility and a known catalyst ahead.

  1. Theta-positive. We collect premium each day. As long as SPX stays above the short strike, time decay works in our favor.
  2. Defined risk. Maximum loss is the width of the spread minus the credit. There is no scenario where we lose more than the spread width.
  3. IV-friendly. Elevated near-term implied volatility means richer premiums. We are selling into the fear, not buying into it.
  4. FOMC-comfortable. We have 9 calendar days before the FOMC decision. The structure can absorb a ±3% SPX move without taking meaningful loss, and we will close the position well before the event (per the time stop below) unless it has already reached 50% of max profit.

Why Not the Alternative Structures

SPY bull put spread. Same structure, but SPY is American-style and carries early-assignment risk on the short put. The credit is roughly equivalent. We default to SPX for cash settlement.

XLK bull call vertical. XLK is the most beaten-up sector this week (-5.5% over 5 days). The temptation is to buy-the-dip on Tech. But the catalyst that drove XLK lower (rate sensitivity into FOMC, geopolitical risk-off) is the same catalyst that would continue to weigh on the sector into next week. Buying XLK calls ahead of FOMC is fighting the catalyst. We would prefer to see FOMC resolve first, then reassess XLK on a relief bounce — and even then, we would default to the broader index for cash settlement.

Iron condor on SPX. Iron condors are excellent in range-bound regimes with elevated implied volatility. The current regime is BULL with a sharp defensive rotation, not range-bound. Iron condors cap both sides of the move; we want to keep the upside open here, which is why we are running a one-sided bullish structure.

Long SPX put or put spread (hedge). This would be the right structure if we were already long a stock portfolio and wanted to hedge tail risk. As a stand-alone trade, it is a directional positioning for a decline that the data does not support — breadth is still healthy at 75% above 50-day.

Long volatility (straddle or strange). Long-vol structures benefit from a vol spike. The term structure is in mild contango (0.914), meaning the market is NOT positioned for a sustained vol spike. Long vol here would be fighting the term-structure signal.

Option Volume and Liquidity

SPX is the most liquid index option product in the world. Average daily volume in the front-month chain runs in the millions of contracts. Bid-ask spreads on the strikes we are using (delta 0.18 and 0.05) are typically $0.05-$0.10 wide. This is the institutional standard for index option strategies.

For comparison: SPY options are also very liquid, but slightly wider spreads than SPX at the same delta. XLK options are tradeable but spreads run $0.15-$0.30 wide at similar delta levels, and volume is roughly 10% of SPX. XLE and XLF have similar liquidity to XLK. For a 1-lot trade, the SPX liquidity advantage saves roughly $5-$10 per side on entry — material on a $1,400 max-profit trade.

If you are trading smaller size and want single-name exposure, the stock alternative (buying 100 shares of SPY or XLK directly) is also viable, but you give up the defined risk and theta-positive structure of the spread.

Trade Management

Following the standard SOP from the Playbook:

  • Profit-take: close the position when it reaches 50% of max profit (approximately $7 credit remaining, or $700 per contract).
  • Stop-loss: close the position if it reaches 2x the credit received in loss (approximately $28 debit, or $2,800 per contract).
  • Time stop: close the position at 7 DTE if it has not reached the profit-take or stop-loss threshold.
  • Adjustment: if SPX drops to within 1% of the short strike with 14+ DTE remaining, roll the short strike down 5 points and out 7 days for a credit. Do not roll down without collecting a credit.

Risks to the Trade

  1. FOMC surprise. If the Fed cuts 50 basis points unexpectedly, the term structure inverts and the volatility regime changes. Our structure would still work as long as SPX stays above the short strike, but we would manage the position through the event rather than hold to expiry.
  2. Iran escalation. A further escalation — for example, a shipping disruption in the Strait of Hormuz — could push Brent above $100. That would be a risk-off event for equities and could push SPX toward our short strike. Position sizing should account for tail risk.
  3. Tech earnings. Several megacap technology companies report this week. A weak guide from any of them could accelerate the tech selloff and drag SPX with it.
  4. Assignment risk. None for this structure. SPX is cash-settled. There is no scenario where the short put is exercised and we are assigned shares of an index we cannot deliver. This is the primary reason we prefer SPX over SPY for index-level strategies.
  5. Gap risk. SPX does not gap the way individual stocks can, but a Sunday-evening futures gap (driven by overseas news) could open Monday materially below Friday's close. We will monitor the open and adjust if the gap exceeds 1%.
